Strong content. No design holding it together

Despite the platform's solid foundation, the experience lacked design consistency, clear communication, and a user journey capable of explaining how the platform worked or how users could gain value from it. The homepage failed to guide first-time visitors, and users were not progressing through the learning funnel, revealing the need to redesign the experience to make the platform's purpose, navigation, and value immediately understandable and trustworthy.

From a WordPress theme to a design system

The platform had no logo. No color palette. No deliberate typographic decisions. A webinar was scheduled for Monday — the first public-facing event under the Resiliencia en una Caja name — and there was nothing to show behind it. That deadline became the forcing function.

I designed a logo mark built on the platform's core idea: a box containing structure, a key suggesting access. The wordmark used Montserrat for the platform name and Helvetica for supporting text. The palette — blue, magenta, lilac — was chosen to signal institutional credibility without feeling academic. From that work I produced a brand manual that could be handed to any collaborator and keep the visual language consistent. The purchased theme became a starting point to move away from, not a final answer.

Purpose, trust, and one clear action

The original homepage described the platform in language written for a grant report. Technically accurate, functionally invisible. A visitor arriving for the first time had no way to understand what Resiliencia en una Caja was, why it existed, or what they were supposed to do.

The redesign was structured around three jobs: communicate purpose immediately — a direct question in the hero replacing the mission statement; establish trust — a partner logo row making the institutional backing visible on the first screen; and make the next step clear — a single CTA pointing to the self-assessment. The six resilience behaviors were rewritten from module titles into plain-language outcomes.

26 questions, 73 submissions. Where the funnel broke

The self-assessment had been live for nearly a year, built in WPForms. Seventy-three people had completed it. None of them received a result. The form had expired without anyone noticing, touch targets on mobile were 23px, and there was no progress indicator — no way for a user to know how far through they were or what completing it would give them.

The rebuild moved to Fluent Forms. The questionnaire was restructured into 13 labeled steps with a visible progress bar, with each question mapped to one of the ten learning modules. The scoring logic was rebuilt so that on completion, a user receives an actual result — a percentage score per pillar, a reading of where their business is strong and where it's exposed. The form went from a dead end to the first meaningful output the platform had ever delivered.

Where data becomes direction

Completing the self-assessment had always felt like submitting to a void. The score page was designed to close that loop — to make the result feel earned and the next step feel obvious.

The page shows a percentage score broken down across the three resilience pillars, color-coded so a user can immediately see where their business is weakest. Below the score, a set of recommended modules surfaces — not the full catalog, but the specific ones most relevant to their result. The score page functions as a conversion mechanism: it takes the data a user just produced and turns it into a personalized starting point.

10 modules. 3 pillars. One personalized path

The full curriculum covers ten modules across three areas: Disaster Risk Management, Business Strengthening and Governance, and Sustainability and Social Responsibility. The challenge was making the catalog feel navigable, not overwhelming.

The self-assessment score determines which modules surface first on a user's learning path. A business that scores low on risk preparedness sees those modules prioritized. Completing a module unlocks a coupon — a small incentive that also functions as proof that someone moved through the material, not just around it.

From broken funnel to working system

What exists now: a live platform with a functioning self-assessment, a score page that delivers a real result, a personalized learning path, GA4 tracking across the full funnel, Mailchimp integration for follow-up, and a brand manual that keeps the visual language consistent across every collaborator.

What I'm proud of: the results screen is the first meaningful output this platform ever gave a user. It was designed and shipped without a developer. What I'd do differently: test the self-assessment with real SME owners before rebuilding it, start the brand process earlier, and redesign the course pages — the work that gets people to the learning path improved; what happens inside it is the next brief.
